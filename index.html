How is this calculated? (the honest methodology)

Each attribute is turned into a 0–100 sub-score using reference distributions (e.g. U.S. income percentiles, adult height distributions, occupational-prestige scales). Those sub-scores are combined with weights that reflect average revealed preferences of the gender you're trying to attract — drawn from mate-preference research (online-dating and speed-dating studies, cross-cultural surveys, and large-scale "pursuit curve" analyses).

The weighted score is mapped through a normal distribution so that a perfectly average person lands near the 50th percentile, then nudged by a market adjustment. It's a heuristic, not destiny. People are not spreadsheets. 💛

For US cities, that market adjustment is not a hand-set rule — it's a Choo & Siow (2006) marriage-matching model estimated on real ACS 2023 microdata (163k married couples). We type people into socioeconomic-desirability deciles (income + education), recover the gains-to-marriage surface from the matching counts, then re-solve the market-clearing equilibrium under your city's sex ratio — so the shift reflects genuine equilibrium spillovers, not a flat penalty. (International cities still use the simpler sex-ratio model.)

Key sources for the weights & curves: Hitsch, Hortaçsu & Ariely (2010), What Makes You Click? Mate Preferences in Online Dating — income/status asymmetry; Bruch & Newman (2018), Science Advances — age-desirability "pursuit" curves; Buss (1989), 37-culture mate-preference study — kindness/ambition ranking; Fisman, Iyengar et al. (2006) speed-dating experiments; U.S. Census/ASEC income & educational-attainment percentiles. Choo & Siow (2006), Who Marries Whom and Why, JPE — the marriage-market equilibrium used for US cities, estimated on ACS 2023 1-year PUMS. Weights reflect average revealed preferences and are deliberately approximate.
